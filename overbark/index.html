Serious instruments. Unserious conclusions.

Every bark, measured — not guessed

Apple's Sound Analysis isolates each individual bark; Accelerate/vDSP then scores its pitch, tone, and inter-bark tempo — the exact cues peer-reviewed research ties to how a bark feels. A live soundwave rides the deck and lights up with each one, even when the dog trots out of frame.

Posture, not just noise

Apple's on-device Vision-framework body-pose model tracks the dog across frames, not a single still — so the reading is never audio alone. What the engine does with that is proprietary; that it reacts to the whole dog is the point.

Scene-aware, sound-aware

The same on-device classifier that isolates the bark also recognizes what's in the scene — a ball, a doorbell, a rival dog — while an ambient-sound classifier keeps watch over nineteen everyday sounds, from sirens to thunder. So the bubble is shaped by the moment, not a dice roll. Exactly how is a trade secret. A well-instrumented one.

Two dogs, one conversation

Point the camera at two dogs and Overbark tracks and reads both — each gets its own side-by-side column of bubbles, and they talk to each other: one hears a car, the other insists it was the wind; one declares the passing bird a crow, the other, a harmless sparrow. Less a caption, more a transcript of a dispute. And when only one of your dogs is in frame, it wonders aloud where the other one got to — by name.

A transcript, not a caption

Barks and quiet thoughts arrive as distinct comic speech bubbles — shapes vary, thoughts set apart from barks — riding a self-clearing conveyor: newest lands at the bottom, older ones rise and fade as the moment moves on. Drag the stack to set where it sits, and it stays there. Choose a bubble style and one of seven harmonized color themes, in light or dark.

A dog's-eye view, entirely on the phone

A compact observation deck reports four reads in real time — mindset, insights, presence, and vocal — showing the pipeline working as it works. Audio analysis, pose tracking, scene recognition, and the bubbles themselves all run on-device. No account, no server, no cloud model, no data leaving the phone. iPhone and iPad, portrait or landscape.

It reads the room. Then it editorializes.

The bubbles aren't a bag of random one-liners. What the dog "says" is shaped by where it is and what's happening. On a walk it reads the pavement like a morning paper — checking the pee-mail, filing complaints about a rival's scent, sizing up every dog that dares pass. At the beach the deadpan cracks into pure joy: the sea as enormous friend-and-foe, the zoomies, a little seawater "for science." A bird overhead reignites the eternal war — small ones are friends, big ones are sky-mafia to be repelled. And when the dog is resting, curled on a lap, it turns quiet and tender: keeping watch while you sleep, remembering the shelter, the best trade it ever made. Hundreds of lines, situational, in character.

The science is real

Overbark's tone model is inspired by peer-reviewed bioacoustics — research that recorded and analyzed real dog barks across everyday situations. The gist: people can read a surprising amount of a bark's emotional tenor from its pitch, tone, and rhythm alone. Overbark listens for those same acoustic cues live from the microphone; what it does with them next is pure entertainment. That's a hint at how a bark is likely to be heard — not a claim about what the dog meant. We supply the instruments. You supply the interpretation.

Overbark is an independent novelty app — not affiliated with or endorsed by any researcher or institution. The bubbles are hand-written jokes and don't represent anyone's findings.

Yes, it uses AI. No, nothing ever leaves your phone.

This is the part people rightly ask about, so here it is plainly. The "AI" in Overbark is Apple's on-device machine learning — the Vision, Sound Analysis, and Speech frameworks — running on your iPhone or iPad's own Neural Engine. There is no cloud service, no server, and no large language model anywhere in it: no ChatGPT, no Claude, no Gemini, nothing of the sort. Your dog's image, its barks, and anything you say are analysed in the moment, on the device, and then let go — none of it is a "prompt," none of it is uploaded, and none of it trains anyone's model, ours included. Even the lines the dog "says" are written by us in advance and chosen right on your phone by a local engine — not generated by a chatbot in a data centre. The app makes no network calls to do its job. There is simply nowhere for your private moments to go.

How to read the instruments

Overbark keeps the camera view clear on purpose — the readouts live in one bottom deck. Four tiles report what the dog is up to right now — its mindset, its insights, who's present, and its vocal read — and, riding above them, a live soundwave shows what it's hearing. Nothing here is decorative; every glyph reflects the dog in the moment.

MINDSET

The little world the dog thinks it's in right now — yard patrol, dinner countdown, nap, guard duty. The tile only lights up once the dog has actually settled on a read; mid-transition, it dims rather than fake one.

The soundwave above the tiles

Above the tiles, a live waveform runs continuously off the microphone — several thin colored strands, each riding its own real FFT frequency band, plus one thick "sum" wave riding on top. It's always moving; it does not wait for a bark.

Each strand is a real frequency band, so the shape of the wave is the actual texture of the sound in the room — not a decorative animation. When a bark lands, it reacts.

On a detected bark: Pitch · Tone · Tempo

The moment a bark is isolated, the header swaps to a pulsing dot and "BARK", and three live readouts appear:

PITCH — fundamental frequency, Hz TONE — the classified bark tone (e.g. playful, alert) TEMPO — barks per second

At the same moment, the wave itself brightens, thickens, and quickens — a visible "hot" state that fades back to the calm gain read a beat later. It's the same VOCAL tile above, shown at full resolution.

Repositioning the bubble stack

The speech bubbles aren't fixed in place. Drag the stack — anywhere in the bubble area — up or down, and the whole conversation follows: older bubbles rise above and clear off on their own once the dog goes quiet, and Overbark remembers where you left it. You're setting the anchor for where new lines appear, not scrolling back through old ones — it doesn't keep a transcript on screen, it keeps a conversation.
