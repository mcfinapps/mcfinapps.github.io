Overbark listens to a dog's bark the way bioacoustics researchers do — measuring pitch, tonality, and tempo against a body of peer-reviewed animal-communication research — and watches its posture the way an ethologist would, using on-device machine vision. Then it puts a cartoon speech bubble over the dog's head and tells you what the moment sounds like. The apparatus is real. The transcript is between you and your dog.

Serious instruments. Unserious conclusions.

Bark acoustics, properly measured

Each bark is isolated by Apple's Sound Analysis classifier, then scored with Accelerate/vDSP on pitch, tonality, and inter-bark tempo — the dimensions the peer-reviewed literature ties to emotional content. Fast-Fourier analysis, in real time, on the Neural Engine, per bark.

Posture, not just noise

Apple's on-device Vision-framework body-pose model tracks the dog across frames, not a single still — so the reading is never audio alone. What the engine does with that is proprietary; that it reacts to the whole dog is the point.

Scene-aware, not random

The same on-device classifier that isolates the bark reads the wider scene, while object detection takes in the surroundings — so the bubble is shaped by the moment, not a dice roll. Exactly how is a trade secret. A well-instrumented one.

Two dogs, one conversation

Point the camera at two dogs and Overbark tracks and reads both, letting one dog's bark and posture inform the other's translation. Less a caption, more a transcript of a dispute.

Augmented, not animated

The translation is pinned above the dog's head in the live camera view and tracks it frame by frame from the on-device Vision read — not a sticker stuck to the screen. Just the annotation, floating where a nature documentary's lower-third would.

Entirely on the phone

Audio analysis, pose tracking, scene detection, and the AR rendering all run on-device. No account, no server, no cloud model, no data leaving the phone at any point.

The science is real

Overbark's tone model is grounded in peer-reviewed bioacoustics research, built from analysis of thousands of dog barks across many real-world contexts. The finding, stated plainly: pitch is the dominant cue humans use to read a bark's emotional tenor, with tonality and inter-bark tempo as supporting signals — and it holds across cultures, ages, and dog ownership. Overbark computes the same features from the live microphone feed and scores them the same way. That tells us how a bark is likely to be heard. It does not tell us what the dog intended. Sounds like something. We report, you decide.

Nothing leaves your phone

Camera, microphone, and (if you switch it on) speech recognition are used only on your device, in the moment. Nothing is recorded, stored, or sent anywhere. No account, no tracking, nothing to collect — like every McFin app. Read the privacy policy →

A note on methodology

Overbark is built on real acoustic research and real on-device machine vision. It is also, functionally, a very well-instrumented guessing machine pointed at an animal that cannot confirm or deny anything it is reported to have said. We stand behind the engineering. We make no claims about the dog.
